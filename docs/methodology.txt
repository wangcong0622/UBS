Overview: 
The study leverages UBS AI LLMs to track the stance of Central Banks’ speakers on monetary policy by examining the language used in their speeches and press conferences. 

The study enables you to:

- Track the overall hawkishness or dovishness for each bank and monitor its evolution over time.
- Identify the topics being discussed (e.g., inflation) and assess their contribution to the overall hawkishness or dovishness index.
- Analyze the contribution of each speaker to the bank’s hawkishness or dovishness index.
- Observe shifts in each speaker’s sentiment (hawkish or dovish) over different periods, both overall and in relation to specific topics.

Methodology: 
For each bank, three distinct LLMs were trained to analyze the language used by its speakers over time. Each speech is first segmented into sentences.

Topics: Identifies the dominant topic discussed in each sentence. Only topics relevant to monetary policy are considered for further analysis. The identified topics include:
- Balance Sheet (including Quantitative Easing & Quantitative Tightening)
- Currency & Exchange Rate
- Employment
- GDP/Growth
- Inflation/Prices
- Monetary Policy/Interest Rates Setting
Sentences where the dominant topic is not among these are excluded from the analysis. Excluded sentences cover finance stability, regulations, supervision, non-core content, and linking phrases (e.g., "Good morning").

Relevancy: Determines the relevancy of each sentence to the hawkishness/dovishness sentiment detection. 

Sentiment: Detects the speaker’s stance towards monetary policy in each relevant sentence on a 5 point scale: 
- Explicitly Hawkish 
- Hawkish 
- Mixed Argument 
- Dovish 
- Explicitly Dovish 
   
Key Metrics: 
Sentiment Index: 
The sentiment scores of sentences are aggregated to calculate a speech-level average score. The overall average score is a weighted sum of relevant sentences classified as Explicitly Dovish, Dovish, Mixed, Hawkish, and Explicitly Hawkish, scored as -1, -0.5, 0, 0.5, and 1 respectively. This sum is normalized by the total number of sentences (both relevant and irrelevant) discussing a monetary policy-relevant topic in the speech.
Example: 
If a speech contains: 
- 150 sentences 
- 100 had a monetary policy relevant topic associated with them. Among those: 
- 35 were relevant for sentiment 
- 65  were irrelevant for sentiment 
- 50 didn’t have a monetary policy relevant associated topic

Next, an exponentially weighted moving average is applied for smoothing purposes. Users can select between 15, 30, and 40 speech windows, with weights decaying exponentially over time. A shorter window is more reactive to recent sentiment shifts but noisier, while a longer window is smoother but slower to react.

Since speeches do not occur daily, speech scores are forward-filled between speeches for continuity.

Caveats: 
- General: 
- National banks speakers (ECB) are not included in the analyses, which only tracks appearances from the Executive Board members (ECB). Press conferences statements and Q&A are not included for BOJ.
- Figures, captions, tables and any sentences not discussing a monetary policy relevant topic are excluded from the analysis. 
- For press conference Q&As, only the speaker’s comments are included in the sentiment score calculations and not the questions themselves. 
- Currency & Exchange Rate sentences are only included for BOJ.
